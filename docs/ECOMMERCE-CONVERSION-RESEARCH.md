## 1. What Strong Ecommerce Sites Repeatedly Do Well

### A. They reduce checkout abandonment directly

Baymard's updated 2025 cart abandonment research still places global average cart abandonment at roughly 70%.

What this means:
- checkout friction still destroys a huge amount of value
- improving checkout flow is not polishing; it is revenue work

### B. They make guest checkout easy

Baymard's research says guest checkout should be a button, not a weak text link.

Why this matters:
- account creation pressure creates avoidable drop-off
- even existing-account users may abandon if they are forced into recovery flows

### C. They support real search behavior

Baymard's 2024 search research shows many sites still fail key query types.

Important takeaway:
- users search by use case, symptom, copied feature text, occasion, and compatibility
- search should reflect actual behavior, not only internal product taxonomy

### D. They remove ambiguity at decision points

Shopify's checkout guidance emphasizes clarity and trust during checkout.

What users want to know:
- when will this arrive
- what happens if there is a problem
- can I trust this store
- what exactly am I agreeing to

### E. They treat product pages as conversion surfaces

Shopify's conversion and product-page material reinforces:
- product pages need clarity, pricing, imagery, trust, and useful detail
- conversion rate depends heavily on whether product pages actually answer the buyer's questions

## 2. Practical Patterns We Should Inherit

### Product discovery
- support browsing plus search
- filters and sorting are mandatory once the catalog grows
- use-case collections can outperform strict category logic in curated stores

### Product pages
- high-quality images
- clear value description
- obvious CTA
- trust details near the action
- shipping/returns/help information visible without friction

### Cart and checkout
- visible guest checkout
- clear totals
- clear shipping timing
- familiar payment options
- minimal forced detours

### Trust
- social proof
- reviews
- real imagery
- clarity over vague reassurance

---

## 3. What This Means For Our Builds

- We should stop treating checkout as the end of design. It is one of the main places where value is won or lost.
- Search should enter the conversation earlier in store builds, especially when the catalog is larger than a very tight curation.
- Use-case navigation and search support are especially valuable in specialty retail.
- Guest checkout should be the default assumption unless a specific business model genuinely requires accounts.
- Product pages need to answer hesitation, not only describe the item.

---

## 4. What Not To Copy Blindly

- do not add giant enterprise filtering to a tiny curated store
- do not add account creation pressure by default
- do not rely on design polish if pricing, shipping, and trust are unclear
- do not assume users search the way the inventory spreadsheet is organized

The lesson is not "copy Amazon".
The lesson is to remove uncertainty where users decide whether to buy.
