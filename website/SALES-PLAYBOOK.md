# Website Sales Playbook

This document captures how to sell websites and website rebuilds in a more repeatable, consultative way.

Use it for:
- outreach
- discovery calls
- proposal writing
- pricing logic
- closing and handoff
- turning one build into a repeatable service

Related research:
- `docs/WEBSITE-SYSTEMS-RESEARCH.md`

---

## 1. Core Sales Rule

Sell the business outcome, not the website artifact.

Most businesses do not want:
- HTML
- a framework
- a redesign for its own sake

They want:
- more trust
- more leads
- better presentation
- easier updates
- clearer conversion paths
- fewer missed opportunities

The website is the delivery mechanism.

---

## 2. Best Sales Model

Use consultative selling.

That means:
1. research first
2. diagnose before proposing
3. tie every recommendation to a business problem
4. present a solution in the client’s language
5. keep the project aligned to the buyer’s real goals

This is stronger than pitching generic design improvements.

---

## 3. Pre-Outreach Research

Before writing or calling:
- review the current homepage on mobile and desktop
- identify the current CTA
- note trust gaps
- note content gaps
- note speed or clutter problems
- note whether the site looks editable or abandoned
- compare against 2-3 better competitors in the same category

Useful outreach angles:
- weak mobile hierarchy
- hidden phone/contact path
- generic template look
- outdated trust signals
- no clear product/service separation
- no usable admin path if the business changes content often

---

## 4. Discovery Questions

Discovery should reveal:
- goals
- blockers
- update frequency
- decision-makers
- urgency
- budget and scope reality

Best website-focused discovery questions:
- What do you want the website to do better in the next 6 to 12 months?
- Where do most customers come from right now?
- What happens after someone lands on the site?
- What are people calling or messaging you to ask that the site should answer better?
- What part of the site feels most outdated or weakest to you?
- Who will need to edit the site after launch?
- How often do products, services, or homepage messages change?
- What would make this project feel successful for you?
- Is this mainly a presentation site, a lead-gen site, or an operational tool?
- Is there a real deadline or business event driving this?

---

## 5. Proposal Logic

A good proposal should include:
- a short summary of the business problem
- the cost of leaving it as-is
- the proposed solution
- scope and deliverables
- timeline
- pricing and payment schedule
- what happens at launch
- what support looks like after launch

Do not make the proposal a design essay.
Make it a decision document.

---

## 6. What To Sell

### Package 1: Fast credibility upgrade

Use for:
- simple local businesses
- weak or outdated brochure sites
- businesses needing a better homepage and contact path fast

Typical scope:
- homepage
- services/about/contact
- responsive rebuild
- metadata
- launch

### Package 2: Full business website

Use for:
- service businesses
- local retailers
- companies with multiple offers or service lines

Typical scope:
- 5 to 8 pages
- stronger conversion structure
- trust and SEO basics
- optional CMS or structured content editing

### Package 3: Operational website

Use for:
- catalogs
- businesses that update content often
- sites that need admin, content management, or workflow support

Typical scope:
- frontend + admin
- auth or gated admin route
- data model
- deployment setup
- handoff and training

### Package 4: Ongoing care

Use for recurring revenue.

Include:
- hosting oversight
- updates
- analytics review
- content changes
- small improvements
- issue support

## 7. Pricing Logic

This section is partly inference from market research and partly from our own current pricing model.

What the market signals:
- many web design projects on Clutch are still under $10,000
- average reviewed project cost is much higher than our current small-site pricing
- market hourly rates can sit far above entry-level freelance logic

What this means for us:
- our current small-business pricing is entry-level, not premium-market pricing
- we can price higher when the work includes strategy, writing, admin, backend, migration, or deployment complexity
- websites with operational layers should not be priced like brochure sites

Pricing rule:
- charge by business impact and system complexity, not by "number of pages" alone

---

## 8. How To Present Value

Translate features into business value.

Examples:
- `better mobile hierarchy` -> easier for visitors to call, book, or buy
- `real photography and trust signals` -> stronger credibility and lower hesitation
- `admin panel` -> staff can update products without paying for every change
- `preview deployments` -> safer approval before launch
- `faster load time` -> better user experience and stronger search visibility

If you speak only in technical terms, many small businesses will not feel the value.

---

## 9. Best Outreach Structure

Short outreach email pattern:
1. show you looked at the real business
2. identify 2-3 specific opportunities
3. explain why they matter
4. mention that you prepared or can prepare a concrete concept
5. ask for a short review call

Best performing tone:
- specific
- respectful
- practical
- not too long
- not full of agency jargon

---

## 10. Best Closing Structure

To close cleanly:
- restate the problem in their language
- show the scope clearly
- reduce ambiguity around timeline and payment
- explain launch and support
- confirm who supplies content, access, and approvals

Good closers reduce uncertainty more than they increase pressure.

---

## 11. What Not To Do

- do not lead with frameworks
- do not promise rankings you do not control
- do not underscope content gathering
- do not offer unlimited revisions casually
- do not give design-level editing controls when content editing is enough
- do not sell a static brochure site when the business actually needs an operational system
- do not ignore deployment, hosting, and handoff in the proposal

---

## 12. What We Should Reuse Automatically

Future sales work should default to:
- audit first
- consultative discovery
- problem-first proposals
- pricing by complexity and business value
- a maintenance or support option
- clear distinction between brochure sites, full sites, and operational website products

This is the baseline if we want the website work to be a repeatable business, not a sequence of disconnected projects.
